Brewing All Grain Pros

Pros

  • Better control over final product
  • Better understanding of beer flavors and impact of ingredients and procedures
  • Opportunity for advanced or experimental techniques (e.g. decoction, first wort hopping)

Brewing All Grain Cons

Cons

  • Brewing process takes longer
  • Initial learning curve to "know your system"
  • Additional equipment is more expensive

Ingredients - Yeast

  • Lager vs Ale
  • Bottom Fermenting vs. Top Fermenting Strains
  • Temperature considerations
  • Ale strains produce esters (sometimes "fruity" or "cloves")
    • Classic example is banana notes in hefeweizen
  • Temperature affects the yeast's flavor characters
  • When a lager brews "warm" it creates diacetyl ("buttery flavor" ); necessary diacetyl rest
  • Lagering requires additional equipment and temp control

Ingredients - Yeast

  • Forms - Liquid vs Dry

  • Know the strain's optimal temperature

1. Preparation

  • Assemble all the necessary ingredients before you begin
  • Know your recipe
  • Measure your hops in separate containers
  • Sanitize equipment
  • Prepare yeast

2. Steeping Grains

2. Steeping Grains

  • Crush the grains at the homebrew supply shop or order them crushed.
  • Heat water to ~150 degrees (hot liquor) or the temp specified in the kit
    • No fermentables will be extracted from specialty grains
    • Heating over 170 will extract tannins (not good)
  • Place crushed grain in steeping bag with a knot
  • Steep at temp for 20-30 minutes or the length that the kit dictates
  • Remove grain from hot liquor; compost the spent grains or make beer bread.

3. The Boil

3. The Boil

  • Wort - (pronounced Wert)
  • A rolling boil is essential to release DMS. How essential this is in a extract brew is questionable.
  • Full boil or partial boil?
  • So then, what is the purpose of the boil in an extract brew? - Hops Utilization!
  • Protein Break (Hot Break)
  • Early malt addition or late malt addition? What are your goals?

4. Hops Additions

4. Hops Additions

  • Find a schedule and stick to it.
  • 60-90 minutes — Bitterness
  • 15-30 minutes — Flavoring
  • 0-15 minutes — Aroma
  • To 'hop sock' or not to 'hop sock'
  • Experiment! Why limit yourself to three traditional additions? Experiment with the interplay of varieties.
  • Hop Stands, Hop Backs, Dry Hopping
  • Alpha Acids and International Bittering Units.

5. Whirlpool

5. Whirlpool and Cooling

  • Purpose — so all the hops particles and protein (hot break) pile up in a tidy pile on the bottom of the kettle
  • Result of the whirlpool - Less "trub" in the fermentation vessel and greater wort yield
  • Cool as quickly as possible for cold break resulting in clearer finished product.
  • Cover with clean tinfoil or sanitized lid. From this point on, sanitation is vital!
  • Wort chiller, ice bath

6. Racking To Primary

6. Racking To Primary

  • Sanitation is vital! Read the directions on the product you buy and be meticulous
  • Use gravity and a racking cane to move the cool wort from kettle to primary fermentation vessel (food grade bucket or glass carboy).
  • Aeration will improve yeast health (strainer is my preferred method) and ensure a quick start

7. Fermentation

7. Fermentation

  • Know your pitching temperature
  • Consider pitching rate, but don't think too hard
  • Consider temperature control and the temperature ranges of your preferred yeast strain
  • Airlocks, overflows, etc.
  • Should you rack to a secondary fermenter?
  • Be patient
  • 2 weeks? 3 weeks? It all depends.
  • Hydrometer Readings
